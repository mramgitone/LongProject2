 tasks associated with audio
  • Add Audio to a Movie After Recording Read more…  You can apply audio to an entire movie after you finish recording it. This includes the ability to select built-in system sounds, recorded audio, or imported files (such as MP3 and WAV files). For example, you might use this feature to play music in the background of a movie. See Adding Audio to Movies.
  • Add Audio (e.g., Narration) to a Movie During Recording Read more…  You can apply audio to an entire movie while you are recording it. This typically involves attaching a microphone to your computer (if necessary) and narrating while you record your actions on the screen. See Recording Movies and Selecting the Audio Input Source.
  • Add Audio to Individual Frames Read more…  You can apply audio to individual frames in a movie. For example, you might use this feature to play recorded narration in order to help explain an action on a frame. See Adding Audio to Frames.
  • Add Audio to/as Objects in a Frame Read more…  You can apply audio to some objects within a frame (such as cursors and buttons). For example, you can apply a double-click sound to a cursor to replace the single-click sound. You can also apply audio as objects themselves. See Adding Audio to Objects and Adding Audio as Objects.
  • Record Audio Read more…  You can easily record audio, such as narration, for your movies. The recorded audio file can be created for an individual frame, an object (e.g., cursors and buttons), or an entire movie. When you record audio in , it is stored as an MP3 file in a folder called "Sounds" where your movie files are located. For buttons, the audio file plays when the user clicks the button. For cursors, the audio file plays when the cursor reaches the end of its trajectory. See Recording Audio.
  • Loop Audio Read more…  You can loop any audio file, causing it to restart when it reaches the end of the playing time. This is useful, for example, if you want to play background music for the entire duration of a movie. You can loop audio for an entire movie, a single frame, and some objects (such as cursors and buttons). See Looping Audio.
  • Edit Audio Files Read more…  After you record an audio file, you can edit the file in several different ways, such as adjusting the volume, deleting sections, and inserting silent areas. See Editing Audio Files.
  • Enable or Disable Sounds When You Record Movies Read more…  If this feature is enabled, normal sounds (such as mouse clicks) can be heard as you record a movie. See Enabling and Disabling Sound for Recording.

Audio Behavior—HTML5 Versus Other Output Formats

audio behavior—html5 versus other output formats

Like the other output formats, you can incorporate audio into your output in many ways. However, the way audio works in is different than in the other formats.

  • In output, audio is "stitched" to different points in time, just as you would find in YouTube videos. In the other output formats, this is not the case. As a result, if you skip forward or backward to begin playing the video at a specific point, the audio will be precisely synchronized with that point in the frame or movie.
  • In output, if you have audio that is longer than the movie itself, the audio stops playing when the last frame is finished. Similarly, if you select the option to pause the timing on a frame, audio applied to the movie or frame stops playing when that frame pauses. For the other formats, the audio continues playing whether the movie ends or a particular frame pauses.
  • In output, the loop audio option will work only if the audio is shorter than the length of the frame or movie (depending on whether you set the loop on the frame or movie).
  • In output, audio is not supported in cases where the user initiates the sound (e.g., by clicking a button).
